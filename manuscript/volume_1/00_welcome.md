# Chapter 0 — The Ship That Needs You

> *"Every great voyage starts before you feel ready.*
> *Climb aboard anyway."*

---

**CRASH.**

The wave hit the ship sideways.

Rain. Thunder. A map flapping loose in the wind.

And there, standing at the wheel —
soaked to the bone, grinning like a maniac,
not even a *little* bit worried —

stood a pirate in a bright red hat.

"TREASURE!" he shouted over the storm.
"It's out there. I can *feel* it."

He spun the wheel hard.

The ship groaned.

A rope snapped.

The sail flopped down like a wet towel
and landed — *fwump* — right on his head.

Silence.

From somewhere under the sail, a muffled voice said:

"...ah. That's a bug."

---

He pulled the sail off his face.

Wiped the rain from his eyes.

And looked straight at —

...you.

"Oh!" he said. "You're here!"

"Perfect timing, honestly. I could really use a hand."

He stuck out a hand — dripping wet — to shake yours.

"Captain Byte. Explorer. Treasure-hunter.
Deeply serious about pizza. 🍕"

He waved at the broken, soggy, half-sinking ship.

"And *this* is the beginning
of the greatest adventure of your life."

"Don't worry about the mess.

The mess is the good part."

---

## The deal

Here's what's going to happen, Builder.

You and I are going to build a game.

A real one.

One you can play.
One you can show your friends.
One that starts as *nothing* — and becomes *yours*.

We won't build it all today.

We'll build it piece by piece.
Adventure by adventure.

And here's the strange, wonderful secret:

the game grows as *you* grow.

The more you learn, the bigger it gets.

Book one. Book two. Book three. Book four.
Same ship. Same game. Just... *more*.

We never throw it away to start over.

Real Builders don't restart.
They *improve*.

---

## A small warning

Now — I have to be honest with you about something.

Our very first version is going to be small.

A little rough. A little silly.

Somebody, somewhere, will look at it and say,
"That's not finished!"

Let them.

Because a tiny thing that *works*
beats a giant thing that only lives inside your head.

Around here, we keep it simple:

**Version 1 beats perfect.**

You'll see why soon.

(There may be donuts involved. 🍩)

---

## The most important thing

One more thing before we sail.

The important one.

You *will* break stuff.

Things won't work. Words will come out wrong.
The computer will throw a little tantrum
and flash a scary red message at you.

Most people panic when that happens.

Not us.

When something breaks, we don't say *"I failed."*

We rub our hands together, lean in, and say:

**"Excellent. Now the conversation begins."**

Because a broken program isn't you being bad at this.

It's the computer *talking to you* —
trying to tell you something.

Our whole job is learning to listen.

(Psst — you just learned the single most important lesson
in this entire book.

Told you the mess was the good part. 😄)

---

Captain Byte leaned in a little closer,
like he was letting you in on a secret.

"This whole voyage? It was built for two brothers.

**Tommy** — who wants to build the future.

**Teppy** — who reads at his own pace,
and misses *nothing*.

If it was built to fit them,
it was built to fit you, too.

Every kind of Builder belongs on this ship."

---

## 🧰 Toolbox Card

**The Builder's First Tool**

It isn't a keyboard.

It isn't a fancy computer.

It's four words:

> *"Let's just start building."*

You can always fix it later.

You can't fix a blank page.

> ### 👋 A quick note for parents & teachers
>
> Welcome aboard — this part's for you.
>
> This series doesn't teach coding the usual way.
> There are no dry lectures here, and no fear.
>
> Every idea shows up because the *story* needs it — so your Builder
> learns to *think*, not just to type. Mistakes are treated as clues,
> never as failures. That one mindset outlasts any programming language.
>
> You don't need to know how to code to help. Sit beside them and ask
> the magic question: *"What is the computer trying to tell you?"* — then
> let them work it out. The pride on their face when they do is the whole point.
>
> Curiosity first. Everything else follows.
