# Chapter 1 — The Map Grows

> *"A dream is not a plan.*
> *But a plan small enough to carry is how a dream gets to sea."*

---

The game was finished.

That was the strange part.

*Captain Byte's Treasure Quest v1* sat on the screen exactly as you shipped it.

Title screen. Five trials. A win ending. A menu that never once crashed.

Finished. Real. **Yours.**

And the crew had been standing around it in total silence for four straight minutes.

"...So," said Captain Byte.

"Here it comes," said Dragon Debug, without looking up from his tea.

"So what if it were *bigger*."

---

## The Dream

It came out of him like water out of a broken barrel.

"A world! Not one room — *rooms.* Ten thousand rooms.

Monsters with *names.* A monster who's having a bad day and takes it out on you.

A **shop.** A shopkeeper who *haggles* — who looks you in the eye and says *no.*

Save files, so you can walk away and come back and it's all still there.

Weather. Sound. A ship you can actually *steer.* A—"

"Captain," said Dragon Debug.

"—a *bestiary*, I've always wanted a bestiary, I don't entirely know what one is—"

"Captain."

Captain Byte stopped, breathing hard, eyes bright.

Nobody laughed at him.

That's worth noticing.

A year ago the crew would have called that list ridiculous.

Now they were all just quietly doing the same math: *how much of that could we actually do?*

---

TommyBot had been silent the whole time, which was unusual.

"There's one you missed," he said.

He reached over and tapped the menu. Trial one. Trial two.

"The chest and the gate still don't know each other.

You crack the chest, you get a number. Then you walk to the gate,

and the gate has never heard of that number.

They're in the same game and they're strangers."

He said it lightly. It didn't land lightly.

He'd been carrying that one since the day he shipped v1 —

that itch, the one he couldn't scratch with anything he knew yet.

"What if the chest *dropped a key,*" he said, "and you carried it to the gate?"

Captain Byte went very still.

"...Say that again."

"What if it wasn't five little games in a list.

What if it was **one world**, and it *remembered you walking through it.*"

Nobody said anything for a moment.

Somewhere under all the noise about weather and bestiaries,

that was the actual dream. That was the one worth the voyage.

"Why bigger, though?" asked Dragon Debug, mildly, into his tea.

TommyBot shrugged, a little embarrassed.

"...I want to hand it to someone," he said. "And watch them play it."

Dragon Debug did not answer that.

He just filed it away, carefully, the way you file away the important things.

---

## Plan First

"Right!" said Captain Byte, already rolling up his sleeves. "All of it. Today."

"No," said Dragon Debug.

Captain Byte's face fell about four inches.

"Not *no* to the dream," said the dragon. "The dream is excellent. Keep the dream.

*No* to starting it at three in the afternoon with no idea what 'it' is."

He set down his tea, which is how you know he means it.

"You built v1 by feel, Captain, and you got away with it —

because v1 was small enough to hold in your head all at once.

Look at that list. Look at it honestly. Can you hold *that* in your head?"

Captain Byte looked at the list.

The list looked back at him. It was enormous.

"...Not even slightly," he admitted.

"Then before we build," said Dragon Debug, "we **plan.**"

"I hate that word."

"I know. Everybody does, until the first time it saves them a week."

"Fine," said Captain Byte. "Fine! We'll plan. How?"

And there it was. The gap.

The crew had a dream the size of an ocean

and not one single person aboard who knew how to chart it.

"We're going to need a bigger map," said Captain Byte.

"The map's fine," said a voice from the dock.

---

## The Navigator

She came up the gangway without waiting to be invited,

which is roughly the most accurate introduction anyone could give her.

She carried a spyglass shaped like an enormous letter **O**,

a logbook thick enough to stop a cannonball,

and the general air of someone who had already read the ending.

"The map is *fine,*" she repeated. "Maps are always fine.

What you're missing is someone who can **read** one."

She lifted the spyglass and swept the horizon, muttering as she went —

not to anybody, just out loud, the way some people hum.

"Harbour's clear... the north strait is faster, but the reef makes it O(n²)

on a bad tide, and I have no interest in an O(n²) afternoon...

south is longer and boring and *survivable*... hm. Boring is underrated."

She lowered the spyglass. Blinked at the crew as if just noticing them.

"Oh! Hello. You're the ones with the enormous list and no route."

"How do you know about the list?" said Captain Byte.

"You've been shouting about a bestiary for eleven minutes, Captain.

The gulls know about the list."

Dragon Debug's tea paused halfway to his mouth,

which is the dragon equivalent of leaping to his feet and cheering.

"Crew," he said, "meet **Lady-O-Query**. The Navigator.

She sees further than anyone I've ever sailed with."

"I see *routes,*" she corrected, delighted. "Anyone can see far.

Seeing far is easy — you just need a good spyglass and a tall mast.

Seeing the *route that doesn't sink you* — that's the job."

She snapped the logbook open.

"Measure the route twice. Sail it once."

---

Then she looked at Captain Byte's dream list, and her eyes lit up like a harbour fire.

"Oh, this is *marvellous.* This is a genuinely marvellous mess.

Right — can I ask a quick question—"

The crew had known her for ten minutes.

They groaned in perfect unison anyway.

**"Oh… *Query!*"**

Dragon Debug didn't groan. He smiled into his tea.

He'd sailed with her before.

---

She asked the question. Then she asked eleven more.

"Ten thousand rooms — is that ten thousand *different* rooms, or ten thousand *the same room*?

Different how? Different text, or different *things in them*?

Do things in a room stay where you left them? Do they stay after you close the game?

Does the shopkeeper have *money*? Can he run out of money?

What happens if you sell him something he already owns?

If a monster is having a bad day, does the *next* monster know about it?

What's for dinner? Fish? Mackerel or tuna? Steamed or—"

"**NAVIGATOR.**"

"Sorry. Sorry. That last one was unrelated."

Captain Byte had his head in both hands.

"That's *twelve* questions and I don't know the answer to *any* of them!"

"Yes," said Lady-O-Query, cheerfully, writing all twelve down.

"That's exactly what I was checking."

---

## The Bug That Isn't In The Code

Here is what happened next, and it's worth watching closely,

because it's the first honest bug of Volume Two

and there isn't a single line of Python in it.

Lady-O-Query started planning.

She was *magnificent* at it.

By four o'clock she had the twelve questions answered.

By five she had a diagram of how a pocket that holds things

would have to talk to a room that contains things.

By six she had a *second* diagram, because the first one had a flaw

that would only have bitten them in about nine months.

By seven she had contingencies. By eight, contingencies for the contingencies.

By nine the logbook was open on the table and it was *beautiful* —

routes and risks and futures, every one of them real, every one of them useful.

The lamps were lit. The tea had gone cold twice.

And TommyBot, who had been quiet for a long time, said:

"...Lady-O?"

"Mm."

"We haven't built anything."

The Navigator's pen stopped.

Dragon Debug said nothing at all. He just let the sentence sit there.

(He could have said it hours ago. He didn't.

He'd been waiting to see who *would.*)

Lady-O-Query looked up. Looked at the harbour, still right there outside the window.

Looked at nine hours of flawless charts for a voyage that had not moved one inch.

"Oh," she said quietly. "Oh, I've done it again, haven't I."

"Done what?" said Captain Byte.

"Planned the whole ocean." She closed the logbook. "Every reef. Every tide.

Every monster. It's all correct, Captain. Every word of it is *correct.*

And it's nine o'clock, and we're still tied to the dock."

She rubbed her eyes.

"My route is perfect. Perfect routes don't sail."

Dragon Debug put down his cup.

**"Excellent. Now the conversation begins."**

---

"You're not *wrong* to plan, Navigator," he said.

"You're doing the right thing at the wrong size."

He turned to the crew — to all of them, and to you, Builder.

"The Captain's bug is that he'd build with no plan at all,

and hit the wall at full speed. You've watched him do it.

The Navigator's bug is the same bug, wearing better clothes:

she'd plan until the wall never gets hit, because the ship never leaves."

"So what's the answer?" said TommyBot. "Plan, or don't plan?"

And Dragon Debug did the thing he does now, instead of answering.

"You tell me," he said. "You shipped a game. What did you actually know

on the morning you started it?"

TommyBot thought about that for a long moment.

"...Almost nothing," he said slowly. "I knew what it *was.*

I knew when it'd be *done.* I found out the rest by building it."

"There it is," said Dragon Debug.

## The Ritual

So they did it the small way.

Not no plan. Not the whole ocean.

**One page.** Plain words. Things you can actually check.

Lady-O-Query turned to a clean sheet — and this is the thing to watch,

because this is the ritual of the entire volume:

**she didn't cross a single dream out.**

"Nothing gets deleted," she said. "Deleting a dream is how you lose it.

We're not deleting. We're *scheduling.*"

She wrote the dream in one line. One. The whole ocean, in one honest sentence.

Then she wrote what "done" would mean — small enough to *prove.*

Then she drew a line across the page,

and put the six honest things above and below it.

```
TREASURE QUEST v2 — THE BLUEPRINT

What we're building:
  A game where the pieces know each other.

Ship first (v1):
  1. an inventory — a pocket that remembers what you picked up

Later (still on the map — just not today):
  2. a shopkeeper who haggles
  3. save and continue
  4. many rooms
  5. a bestiary
  6. colour

Done means:
  The number you crack out of the chest
  can be carried to the gate — and the gate opens.
```

Captain Byte stared at it.

"That's... that's *tiny.* Where's my weather? Where's my ten thousand rooms?"

"On the map," said Lady-O-Query, tapping the word *Later.*

"Right there. Safe. Waiting. Nothing was lost, Captain — I *promise* you nothing was lost.

It just isn't Tuesday's problem."

"One pocket," said Captain Byte, slightly wounded. "One measly pocket."

"One pocket," she agreed, "that *remembers.*

Captain — the chest drops a key. Something has to *hold* the key.

Everything you shouted about this afternoon is standing behind that one door.

Open it, and the rest of the list becomes possible.

Skip it, and none of the rest of the list is even *real.*"

Captain Byte looked at the page again, and something shifted in his face.

"...It's not a small plan," he said slowly. "It's a small *first step.*"

"Now you're navigating," said Lady-O-Query.

She looked back at the six lines and smiled, a little privately.

Because she was the only one at the table who could see it yet:

that little list wasn't a wishlist.

It was the shape of the whole voyage ahead of them.

Every line on it was a place they were going to go.

## 🧰 Toolbox Card — The Small Spec

Before you build a big thing, write down what you're building.

Not a novel. **One page.** In plain words. Three parts:

- **What it is** — one honest sentence. If it takes a paragraph, you don't know yet.
- **What ships first** — the smallest version that's genuinely *worth playing.*
- **What "done" means** — something you can point at and check. *"The gate opens."*

Everything else goes under **Later.**

Not deleted. **Scheduled.**

A plan you can't hold in your hand isn't a plan. It's a second thing to build.

---

## 🏗️ In the Real World

> Grown-up Builders do this every single week, and they have real names for it.
>
> A short document saying what a thing is and what "done" means is a **spec**
> (short for *specification*). The list of what it must actually do is the
> **requirements**. A small chunk of work, written down so a team can pick it up,
> is a **ticket**. And when the idea is big enough to argue about, someone writes an
> **RFC** — a *request for comments* — and hands it round to be poked holes in
> **before** anyone writes the code.
>
> Here's the part people get backwards: professionals don't plan because they're
> cautious. Plenty of them are the least cautious people you'll ever meet.
> They plan because **a small plan is what makes a big thing possible at all.**
> Nobody holds a whole system in their head. Nobody. The plan is where you put
> the parts your head can't carry.
>
> And here's the honest other half — the half that keeps the first half from
> turning into Lady-O's nine o'clock:
>
> **Every plan is a guess.** Written by people who haven't built the thing yet,
> and therefore don't know it yet. Real teams don't handle that by guessing harder.
> They keep the plan **small**, ship **early**, find out what was wrong, and fix
> the plan. The plan isn't a promise. It's the best guess you have this morning —
> and the fastest way to get a better one is to go build something.

## 🚀 One Small Number, Going Up

Pycasso had been in the rafters the entire afternoon.

Nobody had noticed. Nobody ever does.

"Beautiful list," he said, upside down, from somewhere near the ceiling.

"Very sensible. Very *responsible.*" He said *responsible* the way some people say *beige.*

"Do you know what's missing from it?"

"Weather," said Captain Byte immediately.

"The *outrageous,*" said Pycasso, dropping to the deck.

"You dreamed enormous today, Captain, and then you were extremely grown-up about it,

and that was exactly right — but somebody has to remind you what you were dreaming *for.*"

He pushed TommyBot gently aside and typed for about fifteen seconds.

```python
import time
import random

ROCKET = [
    "  /\\  ",
    " |  | ",
    " |  | ",
    "/|##|\\",
    " /||\\ ",
]

FLAME  = "^*'`."
HEIGHT = 18
SPEED  = 0.08

for n in range(3, 0, -1):
    print("\033[2J\033[H", end="")
    print("\n" * (HEIGHT // 2))
    print("   " + str(n))
    time.sleep(0.7)

for altitude in range(0, HEIGHT + len(ROCKET) + 2):
    print("\033[2J\033[H", end="")
    rocket_top = HEIGHT - len(ROCKET) - altitude
    for row in range(HEIGHT):
        if 0 <= row - rocket_top < len(ROCKET) and rocket_top >= 0:
            print(ROCKET[row - rocket_top])
        elif row > rocket_top + len(ROCKET) - 1:
            flame = ""
            for spark in range(4):
                flame = flame + random.choice(FLAME)
            print(" " + flame)
        else:
            print("")
    time.sleep(SPEED)

print("\033[2J\033[H", end="")
print("  ...and it's gone.")
print()
print("  Your dreams lift off the same way:")
print("  one small number, going up, every single frame.")
```

Three. Two. One.

And the ship *went.*

It climbed the screen on a column of fire that grew longer underneath it

with every single frame — four sparks wide, roaring, alive —

up past the middle of the world, up past the top of it,

until the rocket cleared the sky entirely and there was nothing left

but the trail still burning where it had been.

TommyBot didn't move for a second.

"...*How.*"

"Four ideas," said Pycasso, holding up four fingers, insufferably pleased.

"And you own two of them already.

One — **string art.** That rocket is five lines of text in a list. That's it. That's the ship.

Two — **one moving number.** `altitude`. It goes up by one. That's the entire launch.

Three — **the loops.** You've been building those since Volume One.

Four — **clearing the screen** between frames, so fast your eye calls it motion

instead of a hundred separate pictures."

"I don't understand half of that yet," said TommyBot.

"***Yet***," said Pycasso, landing on the word like it was the whole point.

"You've got the loops and you've got the strings. This volume hands you the rest.

By the end of it this will stop looking like magic and start looking like *yours.*"

He was already drifting back toward the rafters. Then he stopped and pointed at the screen —

at the block of text near the top that read `ROCKET = [...]`.

"Until then: don't *watch* it. **Poke** it.

That's not my ship. That's a ship-shaped hole where *your* ship goes. Redraw it.

Make it fat. Make it ridiculous. Give it wings it doesn't need.

And that line under it — `FLAME`? Set it to `"01"` and launch on pure binary exhaust.

This piece isn't finished. What it *becomes* is the part I'm leaving to you."

Then he was gone, humming, and the trail was still burning on the screen behind him.

Lady-O-Query was looking at the last two lines of the output with an expression

Captain Byte had not seen on her face before. Something almost soft.

```
  Your dreams lift off the same way:
  one small number, going up, every single frame.
```

"Well," she said. "*That's* my whole logbook, isn't it. In two lines. How rude."

She turned to Captain Byte and tapped the blueprint — one page, one line above the line.

"That's the secret, Captain. That's all a rocket is. Not a leap. Not one heroic afternoon.

**One small number, going up, every single frame.**

Yours is going up on Tuesday. It's called a pocket."

---

Later, when the deck was quiet, Lady-O-Query noticed the little folded note

in TommyBot's hand — the encoded one, half-read, that had been riding in his pocket

since the Escape Room.

She had approximately forty questions about it.

She asked one.

"Is that something you want *read,*" she said, "or something you want to *keep*?"

TommyBot looked at it for a while.

Then he folded it back up and put it away.

"...Not yet," he said.

"Good answer," said the Navigator, and asked her forty questions about something else entirely.

The map was bigger tonight than it had been that morning.

They had a dream, a page, a line drawn across it,

and — for the first time — someone aboard who could read the route.

Tomorrow the ship would need a pocket.

```python
# The Blueprint

wishlist = ["an inventory", "a shopkeeper who haggles", "save and continue",
            "many rooms", "a bestiary", "colour"]
```
